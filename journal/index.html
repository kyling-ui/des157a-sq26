Research: Visual Thinking Strategies

Original Article →

Slow down, read and look with intent, then interpret and reflect. These are not easy tasks for most people, especially since Tiktok and other social media have fried any attention span we have left. Society has compelled people to think that faster is better, that one must always be in a rush and to “speedrun” life. The article takes a step back and asks us to think with intent, specifically when it comes to images and text. Just like many have commented, analyzing is not a skill that comes naturally, it has to be learned and trained continuously as our knowledge banks grow.

While reading the ten ideas that the article offers, I couldn’t help but relate it back to all the art history classes I’ve taken. In those classes, for most exams, essays, and exercises, we would have to examine an artwork (painting, building, sculpture, artifact, etc.), then relate it back to a bigger idea or connect it to history. Through this, I learned how to examine art and used this thinking whenever I would go to art museums or any gallery.

Always, the first step would be to just look at that piece of artwork and examine everything. To look closely with intent is not easy, but as you do it propels you into questioning. Where should I be looking? What does that mean? How does it connect? As you ask these questions, and perhaps with additional help from context, you are able to answer your own questions, transforming this step-by-step process into an experience in which you further appreciate the artwork you were looking at. So how can we bring this experience over to the digital web?

Through Awwwards, I stumbled upon a Ruinart's website that uses an interactive fresco to tell the story of a champagne house’s history. The website is separate from their main one that sells their champagne. The interactive fresco can be dragged to look around the entire painting, allowing users to closely examine certain parts of the painting. There’s absolutely no context in this layer, forcing one to think, “What the hell am I looking at”. But as you click on certain parts of the site, it brings you to a new page that tells you the history and context of whatever you’re looking at. My favorite part is the side navigation bar. Rather than directly taking you to the separate page as you click on an option, it prompts you to “find” the hidden thing that you clicked. Again, this reinforces the experience of having users question and think about what they’re looking at. Overall, I thought this website was effective in its visual design, but also the user experience / user journey that it has people go through. It goes back to the process I talked about earlier, and in doing so makes the user journey more personal and fun for an individual.

Research: Modals & Overlays

Original Article →

When first thinking of overlays and modals, the first thing that comes to mind is when you access a site and three seconds in, a popup an overlay fades in asking you to sign up for their newsletter or some sort of other membership. However, reading the article, I was reminded that overlays can be much more than just promoting separate information.

Personally, a modal’s greatest strength lies in how it provides designers an extra layer of screen space to design with. Other than promotional value, it can be used for functional value; a space for users to complete certain tasks. The article gave the example of an overlay popping up when users try to post on Twitter. Another example I found was in Spotify, where if users try to edit their playlist details, a modal would pop-up. Again, this is a great strength of using overlays as it saves space but also immediately grabs users attention.

The hardest aspect of designing a modal or overlay would probably be the box-sizing. Just as the article says, the sizing should be just right, not too big, not too small. But actually finding this size depends not only on the viewport, but the content within. Finding the right spacing between buttons, content, padding to get that perfect sizing for various modals is what I believe separates good modals from great ones. But perhaps I’m just overthinking it.

A final question that I had while reading the article came from this particular line, “There are better alternatives for modals and shouldn’t be used on mobile devices”. I agree with the statement, but wondered what these alternatives were. I did some digging and found that, commonly, bottom-sheets replace overlays. On a mobile device, the Spotify overlay for changing playlist details was adapted into a bottom-sheet. A bottom-sheet is a perfect solution to overlays for mobile, especially if users are required to submit information.

Research: Form Design

Original Article →

Forms are so common that best practices of form design have already certainly been established. The article goes over many of them and provides explanations as to why we, as designers, should heed to these established best practices.

One common reasoning and way of designing is to reduce the cognitive load on users when filling out forms. Nobody sane wants to fill out countless boxes of information, so how can we reduce this strain? Multiple steps and progress bars help alleviate transparency for users so they can see how much left they have to fill. Minimizing the use of drop-downs, asking easy questions first, and making a form tab-ready are all other ways to reduce load for users.

Another common reasoning for the best practices knocks two problems at once. 1. Reduce the amount of confusion a user could have when filling the form. 2. This in turn reduces the margin of error, allowing for users to not have to refill out the form again, boosting “fill-rates”. Labels, distinguishing between optional and required, providing a show password option are all practices that solve these problems.

E-commerce requires good design forms in order for companies to land more sales. The more complicated, unattractive, unappealing a check-out form is, the less likely it is for customers to purchase items. Knowing this, I wanted to explore popular brands' check-out forms.

Nike has an interesting one. Right off the bat, I thought that their form had failed as their labels were located within the field itself. However, as I typed the label actually shifted to be within the border of the text field. I prefer the look of inline labels and it saves space as well. Their solution to this was pretty neat. Although Nike's check-out form didn't display all the required fields at once like Shopify does, they break it down to three major categories / steps: delivery, payment, and review. Although the one-page checkout is convenient and fast, having a third step for review helps a lot with reducing error for both sides of the transaction. Although slightly different compared to the one-page checkout common for e-commerce websites, Nike's form design is just as effective.
